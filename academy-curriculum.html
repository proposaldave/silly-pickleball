Silly Pickleball Academy curriculum

Baby Block Class

A repeatable class package for helping newer and intermediate players feel safe, skilled, and fun to play with. The goal is not just better technique. The goal is more Give n Receive: calmer partners, longer rallies, softer hands, and silly moments that lower the guard.

Class promise

Players learn that tiny calm blocks can beat big swings.

Who it is for

Lower-level and developing players who panic when pace comes at them, pop balls up, or feel embarrassed at the kitchen line.

Five-star outcome

Players leave feeling safer, more useful to partners, and more willing to show up again because the class made defense social and funny.

Silly hook

Near the beginning, students stand at the opposite kitchen line, 14 feet away, and hit the ball as hard as they want at Mike Crosby, who wears safety eyewear and demonstrates how small the baby block motion can be.

Class flow

Run the same emotional arc every time.

  1. Welcome and score-safe framing. Make it clear nobody is here to be judged. The room is for calm hands, funny misses, and getting one ball better.
  2. Mike Crosby safety-eyewear hard-hit demo. Mike stands at one kitchen line, students stand 14 feet away at the other kitchen line, hit as hard as they want, and watch him baby block the ball with a tiny calm motion.
  3. Kitchen-line baby block. Players try to get the ball by the pro from kitchen line to kitchen line while the pro baby blocks and lets out-balls go.
  4. Five-hole challenge. Players aim through the middle gap. The silly target makes touch memorable and lowers pressure.
  5. Feet target transfer. Players block to the opponent's feet so defense becomes a way to give partners a better next ball.
  6. Live-play invite. End by pairing people who should play again and naming the next class, social, or practice path.

Curriculum modules

Each class module needs skill, silly moment, and connection purpose.

Baby block basics

Ready position, quiet paddle, short motion, firm enough grip, and no panic swing.

Ball-machine out-ball game

Use a fun ball-machine drill where players practice letting out-balls go instead of touching every ball.

Block to feet

Turn defense into a generous ball that helps the team, not just survival.

Next class package

Mastering the Resets

This class teaches players to survive pressure and give their team a playable next ball. The silly moment is that players get to stand at the kitchen line and hit the ball as hard as they want, including overheads, while the pro defends and demonstrates resets from the transition zone.

Who it is for

Players who panic from the transition zone, pop balls up, or feel stuck between the baseline and kitchen.

Silly hook

Students get permission to unload on the pro while the pro turns chaos into soft reset examples.

Five-star outcome

Players leave believing they can handle pace, protect partners, and move forward with more calm.

Score-Help Social

Use score confusion as the joke and the relief valve: staff normalizes 7-11-2, side-outs, and asking for help.

Ball Striking That Works With My Technique and Ability

Build a class where players learn contact, timing, and swing choices that fit their body and level instead of copying one generic stroke.

Belgian Backhand Class

Dave teaches the forehand-on-the-backhand-side shot. It can work for swatting hit balls and as Dave's advanced speedup specialty, but if the ball gets lower players need a real backhand.

Cover the Middle + Tic Tac Toe

Run a tic-tac-toe target drill where teams have to protect the center square, call mine/yours early, and recover when both players freeze or both chase the same ball.

Let It Go Out-Ball Class

Greg Bennett uses the ball machine to feed tempting out-balls. Players score by letting the ball go, calling it confidently, and laughing at how hard it is to do nothing.

Trick Shots Lab

Package the net-cord / Caleb blow shot, Erne, and around-the-post shot into one playful class where advanced-looking shots become safe experiments.

Pro Plays In

Use kitchen lobs, easy balls, and playful resets so members get to attempt finishable balls without feeling judged.

Community Etiquette

Teach proper and generous line calls. If you call an in ball out, your friends will see it and it is a buzz kill. Generous calls help the vibe.

Trick Shots Lab moments

Film one clip for each moment: tapping the ball over the net cord and blowing on it near the tape, stepping into an Erne, and chasing a wide ball into an around-the-post attempt.

Pro assignment map

Give each class type a named NEPC pro example.

Mike Crosby: Baby Block

Baseball-player energy makes the 14-foot hard-hit demo believable: students can unload from the opposite kitchen line while Mike wears safety eyewear and baby blocks.

Ryann Foster Da Silva: Mastering the Resets

Use the Academy Director and APP Pro as the serious skill anchor for defending kitchen-line overheads from the transition zone and turning chaos into soft reset examples.

Caleb Herrick + Ryann Foster Da Silva: Trick Shots Lab

Caleb owns the net-cord / blow-shot silliness. Ryann brings APP Pro credibility to the Erne and around-the-post shots so they feel aspirational, safe, and fun to attempt.

Tina Lech: Score-Help Social

Make score confusion feel safe, social, and normal for newer players who are anxious about slowing everyone down.

Cobi Moore: Ball Striking

Frame ball striking around the player in front of you: what works with their technique, body, timing, and ability today.

Dave: Belgian Backhand

Teach the advanced specialty shot honestly: forehand on the backhand side can swat and speed up high balls, but low balls require a real backhand.

Ben Herrick: Cover the Middle + Tic Tac Toe

Use clear court geometry and simple communication rules so the drill feels like a game: win the center square, call early, and stop letting balls land between partners.

Riley Stockwell: Pro Plays In

Use playful kitchen lobs, easy balls, putaway chances, and confidence-building rallies members can laugh through.

Greg Bennett: Patience

Turn patience, soft hands, long dinking rallies, and ball-machine out-ball restraint into a social rhythm players want to repeat.

Casey Taitel: Community Etiquette

Lead the line-call conversation as a vibe class: generous calls, benefit of the doubt, and keeping friends wanting to play with you.

Fill and follow-up

Every class should create the next invitation.

Before class

Invite the anxious player by name: this is a safe class for learning soft hands, not a competitive test.

During class

Notice who laughed, who relaxed, who helped someone else, and who should be paired again.

After class

Send the next invite: another Baby Block, Belgian Backhand Class, Mastering the Resets, Let It Go Out-Ball Class, Trick Shots Lab, Patience, Score-Help Social, or Pro Plays In.
