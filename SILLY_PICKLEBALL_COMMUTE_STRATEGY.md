# Silly Pickleball Strategy Memo

Audio version for Dave.

Date: June 12, 2026.

## The Core Point

Silly Pickleball is not really about being silly.

It is about using the natural silliness of pickleball to help people let their guard down, feel good, connect with each other, and want to come back.

In a social pickleball club, the real goal is the feeling everyone gets.

I feel good.

You feel good.

We feel good together.

That is the standard.

If an event creates that feeling, it is valuable.

If it does not create that feeling, then it may still be court usage, instruction, or exercise, but it is not the real social club product.

The event is only valuable if people connect.

That is the simplest sentence.

Everything else should be judged against it: programming, matchmaking, invitation, class design, host behavior, pro behavior, follow-up, and the way the club talks to players.

Every suggestion, program, match, invite, and follow-up should be judged by whether it creates more of that feeling.

## What Silly Pickleball Really Is

Silly Pickleball is the operating strategy for building a five-star social pickleball experience.

The target player is not only the serious tournament player.

The target player is the connection-first player.

The lower-level player.

The player who is nervous about being good enough.

The player who worries about slowing people down.

The player who wants to improve, but also wants to feel safe, wanted, and included.

The player who may not say, "I want connection," but who absolutely responds to being welcomed by name, matched with the right people, helped by generous players, and invited back into the next thing.

For that player, the five-star experience is not just good instruction.

The five-star experience is:

They felt safe to show up.

They felt wanted by name.

They were matched with the right people.

The room had hosted energy.

They were helped up by the community.

They were personally invited back.

That is the real product.

Not the logo.

Not the word "silly."

Not just the class.

The product is the repeatable experience of feeling better because you came.

## Why Pickleball Is Perfect For This

Pickleball is already silly.

The name is silly.

The ball is a plastic wiffleball.

Adults are taking a toy-looking ball extremely seriously.

The court has a place called the kitchen.

Dinking is a real word.

The score is confusing.

People argue over 7-11-2 like it is a math emergency.

The game goes from soft-soft-soft to sudden chaos in half a second.

The ball clips the net, dies, and everyone performs apology theater.

"Who's got middle?" becomes a social crisis.

Someone can miss the easiest sitter in the world.

Someone can win a point with a terrible-looking shot.

A 70-year-old can humble a former athlete.

A beginner can accidentally do the right weird thing.

DUPR turns a social sport into a tiny identity crisis.

The sport is full of tiny moments of apology, teasing, patience, encouragement, blame, generosity, forgiveness, and relief.

Those moments are not distractions.

They are the opening.

They drop people's guard.

They let people laugh.

They make it safer to be imperfect.

They create Give n Receive moments.

That is why Silly Pickleball can work.

We are not inventing the silliness.

We are noticing it, naming it, teaching through it, and turning it into repeatable community programming.

## The Core Programming Strategy

Silly Pickleball should become a library of repeatable event formats and class formats.

Each one should have:

A clear promise.

A target player.

A silly moment.

A pro or host script.

A skill progression.

A video example.

A follow-up invitation.

A way to measure whether the event created connection.

The first formats are already becoming clear.

Score-Help Social makes score confusion safe and normal.

Baby Block Class teaches calm soft hands through a funny safety-eyewear demo where players hit hard from the opposite kitchen line and the pro calmly baby-blocks the ball.

Five-Hole Baby Block gives players a silly target and makes touch memorable.

Mastering the Resets lets players hit overheads from the kitchen line while the pro defends from the transition zone.

Let It Go Out-Ball Class teaches that sometimes the best shot is doing nothing.

Trick Shots Lab makes the net-cord blow shot, Erne, and around-the-post shot playful instead of intimidating.

Belgian Backhand Class lets Dave teach the forehand-on-backhand-side specialty shot and when it works or fails.

Cover the Middle plus Tic Tac Toe teaches communication through a game instead of a lecture.

Patience turns long dinking rallies into social rhythm and emotional restraint.

Pro Plays In uses kitchen lobs, easy balls, playful resets, and confidence-building rallies.

Community Etiquette teaches the social rules that actually make the court feel good.

These formats are not random.

They are how the brand becomes real.

## The Etiquette Class Matters More Than It Sounds

Community Etiquette should be a real class.

The biggest note right now:

A bad line call in social play is a MAJOR BUZZ KILL every time.

Whenever you are generous with a line call, that automatically boosts the positive vibes on the court, every time.

This is not a small manners point.

This is a core social-club skill.

People see bad calls.

They feel them.

They remember them.

If a player calls a ball out that was in, the whole court feels worse.

If a player gives the benefit of the doubt, the whole court feels better.

That is exactly the kind of Give n Receive behavior Silly Pickleball should teach.

The etiquette class should include:

Generous line calls.

Benefit of the doubt.

Apology theater after net cords, body balls, mishits, and accidental winners.

Paddle taps as a reset ritual.

Score patience, especially for newer players.

Mixed-level generosity.

Partner protection and early communication.

How to win without draining the room.

The key lesson is simple:

The best players to play with are not always the highest-rated players.

The best players to play with are the ones who make the court feel better.

## The Skill Curriculum Has To Support The Feeling

This cannot become a pure party brand.

The teaching has to be real.

The skill engine underneath Silly Pickleball is 4D Pickleball.

Players become more fun to play with when they have range.

They can hit soft and hard.

They can defend and attack.

They can reset and speed up.

They can invite rallies and finish points.

They can give their partner a better next ball.

That is why the teaching system should not create one-dimensional bangers.

A one-dimensional banger may win some points, but can make the game less fun for everyone.

The better goal is to maximize Give n Receive.

Become more valuable to partners.

Become more fun to play with.

Become more adaptable.

Learn soft when soft helps.

Learn hard when hard helps.

Learn when to let the ball go.

Learn when to reset.

Learn when to attack.

Learn when to keep the group in the rally.

The goal is not just improvement.

The goal is improvement that makes the whole court feel better.

## The Sales Process

The sales process is not posting an event and hoping.

The sales process is understanding who the event is for, why they would trust it, who should invite them, and what next invitation the event creates.

After every event, ask:

Why did this fill?

If it filled, what worked?

Was it the personal invite?

The trusted host?

The right level?

The right vibe?

The right promise?

The right timing?

A friend referral?

A format players already understood?

If it did not fill, what was missing?

Was the audience unclear?

Was trust too low?

Was the level wrong?

Was the benefit vague?

Was the invitation too cold?

The next sales move might be a warmer invite, a smaller test, a bridge player, a better title, a clearer promise, or a direct referral ask.

Every event should create the next invitation.
